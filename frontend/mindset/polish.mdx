# Polish

Polish is not a phase you add at the end. It is a standard you apply throughout. The first version should already aspire to the polish target; "polishing later" usually means never.

<Note>
**Polish is a standard, not a stage.** The first commit holds the polish bar the final commit holds.
</Note>

The reason is mechanical: once a UI ships at "good enough," every subsequent change is measured against "good enough." The bar resets. Raising it later means redoing the work you already shipped.

## The polish dimensions

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Spacing rhythm">
    A consistent base unit (4px, 8px, or whatever the tokens specify) respected throughout. Visible scale, not arbitrary numbers. When spacing values are 7, 13, 18, 22 — there is no rhythm. When they are 8, 16, 24, 32 — the eye reads order even before the user names it.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Alignment">
    Visual alignment, not just CSS alignment. Things that look like they line up actually line up. Optical adjustment matters more than algorithmic — a perfectly centered icon often needs a 1px nudge to feel centered, because shape, not bounding box, drives perception.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Type hierarchy">
    A clear scale of sizes and weights, not nine different font sizes scattered randomly. Two or three weights, three or four sizes, well-paired. Hierarchy reads at a glance when the scale is small; it disappears when every heading is its own size.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Motion timing">
    Micro-interactions feel right around 150 to 250ms for most transitions. Anything faster is invisible; anything slower feels sluggish. Motion has a purpose — confirming state, drawing attention, masking latency — not decoration.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Easing curves">
    Linear easing is the rookie tell. Real polish uses ease-out for entrances (things arrive softly), ease-in for exits (things accelerate away), ease-in-out for state transitions (smooth on both sides). Linear says "I used the default."
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="State coverage">
    Hover, focus, active, disabled, loading, error — all designed. Not afterthoughts. Most amateur UIs cover the default state and 60% of hover, then ship. The other states leak through later as bugs.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Empty states">
    What does the UI look like with zero data? With one item? With a thousand? Polish covers this; amateur work shows a blank screen and calls it done. The empty state is often the first state a user sees.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## The polish review

Read the UI like a critical reviewer. Where does the eye stop? Where does it stutter? What feels off but you cannot articulate why? That last question is the most useful one — the inarticulate "off" is almost always a polish dimension you have not named yet.

Walking through every dimension above, on every screen, is the work. See [Design review](/frontend/workflows/design-review) for the structured pass.

## The temptation

<Warning>
Shipping at "good enough." Polish is the difference between something that works and something that feels right. Users do not articulate the difference but they feel it — and they remember it.

The cost of polish at the start is small. The cost of polish retrofitted is enormous, because every subsequent change has to respect the lower bar you already set.
</Warning>

## Polish across waves

Polish target generally stays constant across [waves](/backend/workflow/waves). Pro UI is pro UI — a W1 MVP should not look like a W1 MVP, it should look like a finished product at W1's scope.

What scales is the breadth of polish:

- **W1** — every shipped screen polished, but the screen count is small.
- **W2** — every shipped screen polished, plus all variants (loading, empty, error) on the critical paths.
- **W3** — full state coverage everywhere, motion specs documented per component, polish bar enforced in review.
- **W4** — polish becomes a discipline with its own audit cadence and consistency checks across the whole surface.

The bar does not move down for early waves. The surface area grows as the wave grows.
